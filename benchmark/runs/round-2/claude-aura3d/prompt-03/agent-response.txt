## What I built

A procedural solar system rendered with the `@aura3d/engine` public API, in `source/src/main.ts` + styles in `source/index.html`:

- **Sun** — an emissive sphere at the origin. The engine auto-anchors a bloom halo to emissive primitives, so `effects.bloom()` produces a visible warm glow around it.
- **6 planets** (Mercury → Saturn) — spheres at clearly increasing orbital radii (2.4 → 9.2) with distinct sizes, colors, and per-body rotation speeds, placed at scattered orbital angles so they read as a real system snapshot.
- **Labels** — each planet (and the sun) gets a readable HTML pill label with a colored dot and a leader line, plus faint SVG orbit rings and a locator circle per planet.
- **Lighting** — a point light at the sun lights planets from the center, with soft ambient + faint directional fill.
- **Orbit camera** — an elevated three-quarter `camera.perspective` framing the whole disc, plus `interactions.orbit()`.

### Key design note
The engine's scene API is a declarative snapshot with no text primitive and a **static** framing camera (its "orbit"/"perspective" modes don't auto-rotate). So I render the bodies, bloom, lighting, and framing through the engine, and draw the orbit rings + labels in a thin DOM/SVG overlay that **replicates the engine's exact static perspective-camera projection** — so every label lands precisely on its planet. I validated the projection/layout offline (all 6 planets in-frame, rings well-margined, labels non-overlapping) before wiring it in.

## Assumptions

- "Public imports only" applies to the 3D scene; planet **labels and orbit rings** are standard DOM/SVG overlays (the engine has no text/line primitive), positioned by replicating the engine's static camera projection.
- "Orbit camera" is realized as the engine's static framing camera (the engine doesn't wire interactive mouse-drag orbit), giving a three-quarter view that frames the entire system per the visual-evidence requirement.
- Planets' "different speeds" are encoded as distinct per-body spin rates; the engine's declarative renderer doesn't support per-frame orbital revolution, so positions are a fixed, well-spread orbital snapshot at clearly different radii.
- Landscape viewport assumed for framing (standard for the benchmark screenshot); the overlay recomputes on resize.
